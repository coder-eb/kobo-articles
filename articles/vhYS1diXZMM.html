Plato’s Warning About Writing

On college campuses around the country, there are these new clubs that are forming and they’re calling themselves Luddites. One of these clubs is at Temple University and they advertise mostly with flyers. Actually, on one of these flyers they wrote, “Do you desire a healthier relationship with technology, especially social media? The Luddite club welcomes you and your ideas.” These students are throwing away their smartphones and getting flip phones instead. Sometimes they’re meeting outside so that they can only use natural light and generally they’re trying to figure out what our relationship with modern technology should be.

This is a new kind of movement, one that I think we’re going to see more of because my suspicion is that we are now living in a time of techno-pessimism. Where generally we saw technology as something that only improved our lives. Every new piece of technology was a bit of progress. People are starting to ask difficult questions about technology. They’re looking around at the world that we’ve built with this technology and sometimes they’re saying, “No. I don’t want that.”

There’s a long history of philosophers writing about, criticizing, and even resisting new technologies. And in fact, it goes all the way back to Plato. Plato wasn’t concerned with, you know, smartphones or the internet when he was writing about technology. When Plato writes about technology, he’s writing about writing itself.

In Plato’s Phaedrus, Socrates tells the story of Thoth and Thamus. Thoth is an Egyptian god who is associated with wisdom, with arithmetic, and importantly for this story, with the invention of writing. In this story, Thoth brings Thamus a range of new inventions and he promises that each one is going to make the Egyptians’ lives better. Thoth kind of says what all technologists say, that this is going to improve the human condition. And Thamus sees some of them and he approves of some of them and he disapproves of others.

But Socrates lingers on the presentation of writing. Thoth promises that writing would make the Egyptians wiser and it would improve their memories. But this is where Thamus says that he’s gone too far. Thamus actually compares Thoth’s uh perspective to that of a parent who can’t see the faults in their own child. And so he’s going to need someone else to take a look at his invention and tell him where things could go wrong. And Thamus says that writing won’t improve our memories, in fact, it will increase forgetfulness. He concludes his criticism by saying that the Egyptians will be hearers of many things and will have learned nothing and that they will have the show of wisdom but not the reality.

Socrates goes on to compare writing to painting. Uh it was saying that painting takes something that’s alive and creates a copy of it, but the copy, unlike the original, is dead. It lacks some of the crucial vital features of the original. And he thinks writing is a lot like this because you can’t ask a text a question, you can’t, you know, really interrogate it. No matter what you say back to a text, it is always going to be saying the same thing. Speech is alive, Socrates is saying, but writing is dead.

As with all of the Platonic dialogues, it can be difficult to figure out, you know, what Socrates’ point is or what Plato intends to teach. It’s difficult, if not impossible, to know what the historical Socrates believed. After all, Plato is using him as a character and we shouldn’t assume that whatever Socrates says in the dialogues is actually what Socrates, like the historical Socrates, would have believed. And we can’t consult any of Socrates’ writings because he never wrote anything down.

There is a historical document called the Lives of the Eminent Philosophers that gives short biographies of important philosophers, particularly from ancient Greece. And Socrates has an entry there. And it’s mentioned that Socrates wrote two things. One of them was a hymn of praise to the gods, but its authenticity was disputed even in the, you know, ancient world. And the other is a fable. Uh there’s a mention of a fable in another Platonic dialogue, the Phaedo, where Socrates mentions that in between being sentenced to death and actually being executed, he has spent his time creating a verse based on one of Aesop’s fable. And primarily, the Socratic legacy is an oral tradition.

Socrates taught many philosophers and they would go on to found schools of philosophy in Greece. Plato, of course, is the most famous example of this. Socrates preferred a dialogical model of doing philosophy. That’s what we see throughout the dialogues. He’ll be at a party or he’ll be in a marketplace or he’ll just find someone walking to court and he’ll start asking them questions and all of the philosophical inquiry happens in the back and forth. You know, a definition is proposed, he asks a difficult question, and then they see if they can revise the definition.

A famous work like The Republic, which is centered on simply one question, you know, why be just or why be moral, is an extended conversation between Socrates and several interlocutors, particularly Glaucon, Thrasymachus, and Adamantus. And perhaps Socrates believed that by writing down philosophy, you would lose this dialogical character of the act itself. And so he never did. And while his student Plato did go on to write down, you know, some of his philosophy, that’s why we have the dialogues, there is a reference in Aristotle to the unwritten teachings of Plato, suggesting perhaps that there were some parts of Platonic philosophy that needed to be passed down orally rather than being written down.

So we see this early skepticism about a new technology. Now, something weird is already happening here because you might think it’s strange to call writing a technology. It’s just part of what human beings do, right? But this is actually a weird fact about technology in general, that technology, as it becomes more enmeshed in our lives, fades into the background and we don’t even think of it as technology anymore.

But as Walter Ong writes in his book Orality and Literacy, the development of the technology of writing transformed human consciousness. Particularly, it transformed how we thought about reason and memory. Ong is praising writing in that book, particularly because writing allows us to store information so easily, and so we can just retrieve it whenever we need it. It doesn’t have to take up much cognitive space. But, he notes that there are downsides. They’re the same sort of downsides that are mentioned by Plato and Socrates, namely our relationship to our memory changes. We become more forgetful because we can rely on things like writing and increasingly things like hard drives to store information for us. We didn’t practice remembering, and so in some ways we’ve forgotten how to do it.

One way to understand this point is to think that every piece of technology comes with some kind of benefit and some kind of cost. And as we’re wondering what our relationship with technology should be, we should compare the cost and the benefits. And we can ask ourselves just this question, is this piece of technology worth the cost?

Socrates seemed to think that writing wasn’t worth the cost. I think most of us would disagree with Socrates on that point and think that in fact writing was a civilization-building tool, and it transformed human consciousness in such a way that we wouldn’t want to go back. But we should still be aware of the fact that it did carry a cost, and every new piece of technology does.

The advent of the calculator made us worse at doing, you know, arithmetic in our heads, right? It’s an easy example. It’s an example everyone likes to bring up because for some of us it happened in our lifetime. As soon as you had a calculator in your pocket at all times, you didn’t have to worry about things like basic division and subtraction and multiplication.

Neil Postman develops some of these themes in his book Technopoly, where he writes that technology has an ecological impact. It’s not only that technology adds something to our lives, it’s that it transforms the environment around us. And I don’t think it’s an exaggeration to say that technology can transform how we see the world. And this can be a good thing, but of course it can also be a bad thing.

That’s the theme I want to explore in the rest of this video. How does technology shape us? How does technology shape how we see the world? And the new Luddites that we see on college campuses are asking exactly these questions. And that’s what I find so interesting about them.

The Original Luddite Movement

But the roots of this movement actually go back pretty far in history. First, we have the original Luddites. Now, if you’ve encountered the term Luddite before, it’s probably been as an insult. We use it as a description of someone who is maybe too stubborn or too stupid to adopt whatever the latest technology is. And it can mean that someone is regressive and that they can’t keep up with the times. Though there are some people who would proudly call themselves Luddites. But this idea of someone just being stubborn and regressive, I don’t think it’s a very good description of the original Luddites.

I wanted to look into this a little bit and so I read from Brian Merchant’s book Blood in the Machine, which is about in large part the Luddites. The Luddites emerged during the Industrial Revolution in England. And at the center of all of these issues was the wool industry, which made up a huge part of the English economy at the time. And because of new innovations like the power loom and eventually steam power, we saw a huge disruption in the lives of these workers. And they found themselves being automated out of a job. And there were people who were going to get very rich because of this, but they weren’t the workers themselves.

Around this time we also saw the rise of the factory system. And these factories were seen as highly oppressive places. Orphans were sometimes recruited to come and work in these factories. And they would be promised really nice lives. They would be promised nice, you know, filling meals, a place to sleep. Sometimes they would even be promised that they could ride horses. And then if they worked until they were 21, they would be able to enter into the workforce and work as professionals. That was the promise.

In some factories that were in Cromford, they employed about 2,000 workers and nearly 2/3 of those workers were children and some of them were as young as six years old. Merchant describes the experience of these orphans in some length in his book. And one image that was particularly striking to me was the orphans being delivered to the factory in the first place. People would ride around and try to recruit these orphans making these promises to them and then they would put them in basically cages to transport them there. And town people would watch them and you know with looks of sadness on their face because they knew what terrible lives these orphans were going to live.

What had previously been a place where, you know, some skilled workers could make decent livings became a place that primarily was hiring orphans and was treating them terribly. And if you were living through it at the time and you’re seeing these new technologies arrive like the power loom, like steam power, like the factory system, it’s hard to look at that and say, “Yes, this is progress.”

And it’s around this time that the story of Ned Ludd starts to get told. Now, Ned Ludd was a mythic figure. I don’t believe there is any historical evidence that he actually existed. He’s a bit like Robin Hood in this regard. But the story that was told about Ned Ludd was that he was learning to use a knitting frame and his master told the magistrate that his apprentice was being lazy and so Ludd was actually ordered whipped. After he was whipped, he was so angry that he smashed the knitting frame and then he flees to Sherwood Forest. And workers followed his example of smashing the knitting frames. Sometimes they would destroy, you know, the machines, sometimes they would set fire to the factories and they would band together in these groups and they would write letters explaining why they were doing what they were doing and the leaders would always sign the letters with the name General Ludd.

This, as far as I know, was the first kind of big revolt against big tech. We can draw a little bit more on Neil Postman to see what was happening during the Luddite movement. The invention of these automated technologies and factories, I would say encouraged the owners of the factories, the people who controlled the factories, to start viewing their workers like parts in a machine. And the thing about being parts in a machine is that when one of them breaks or doesn’t work that well, you just throw it away and you replace it.

Well, so one way to understand the Luddites, I would say, were people who were standing up for human dignity in the face of technological innovation. So, we’re going out a little bit out on a limb here, but if I’m right, then we wouldn’t just say that technology shapes how we view the world around us. Technology can also shape how we view ourselves.

Heidegger and the Enframing of Reality

Okay, we’re not going to linger on the history stuff much longer. In fact, I want to go back into some philosophy to try to go deeper to the heart of the issue. Um and one piece that was really useful for me as I was thinking through these issues was a famous essay from Martin Heidegger called the question concerning technology. Now, I’m going to admit that I am basically an amateur when it comes to reading Heidegger. He is so far out of the tradition that I am used to reading um that I can find it quite difficult. You know, I’ll put up a quote here on the screen where I would say, “I don’t really know what Heidegger means when he writes something like this.”

But there’s one piece of jargon that comes from this essay that I found quite useful. And this is what Heidegger calls enframing. Heidegger argues that technology conditions us to view the world around us as a kind of standing reserve or a collection of resources that can be extracted and used and exploited. One of his favorite examples is a power plant that is built on the Rhine. So, once the power plant is built, our relationship to the Rhine changes. It is no longer this thing of unimaginable power and beauty. Instead, it is a standing reserve which is ready to generate more power for our in mostly industrial uses.

Heidegger is going a bit deeper than a typical point that you would make about technology, which is that technology allows us to do more things. That’s true, Heidegger would say, but technology doesn’t only allow us to do more, it changes our general relationship with the world around us, and we come to view the world differently. I think for Heidegger, he would worry that this cuts us off from more primordial ways of connecting with nature. Um and this might also be a kind of theme that you would see in people like the German Romantics, who really felt like getting in touch with nature was a way of overcoming alienation.

And while I can’t claim to be an expert in Heidegger, in fact I’m pretty much an amateur, and I really do struggle to understand parts of this essay, I think that this concept of enframing is a useful one. And the question I’m particularly interested in the rest of this video is what happens when we come to view ourselves as a kind of resource that can be extracted. Heidegger’s focus really is the land around us, but we have technologies that allow us to track, measure, and extract from ourselves. And so that would mean our relationship to ourselves would also change. So we can ask ourselves this question, when it comes to how we view ourselves as human beings, what does modern technology make easy and what does it make hard?

Attention, Metrics, and Value Capture

There’s an organization called the Friends of Attention, which is a collection of artists and academics and writers who all call themselves attention activists. They recently published a book where they outlined their beliefs, and that’s called Attensity. One of the key moves in Attensity is just naming the phenomenon that we are experiencing when we plug ourselves in to modern machines. And the name that the Friends of Attention give it is human fracking. While fracking in the energy sector, you know, destroys woods and waterways, so it does damage to the external environment, human fracking does damage to our interior environment, they say. And the Friends of Attention think that we have to pay just as much, if not more, attention to our interior environment, because if we don’t spend any time focusing on our interior environment, and cultivating it, and protecting it, then we can never even get around to really caring or fixing the issues that are out in the exterior world.

The Friends of Attention are largely concerned with fixing our attention span, something I’ve talked about dozens of times on this channel, and we’re not going to focus too much on attention spans in this video. But one thing I want to note is that they think of attention in a very different sort of way than you would typically encounter it in something like a video on YouTube.

One of the academics that makes up the Friends of Attention is D. Graham Burnett, who is a historian of science. He’s written extensively on the history of attention research, and how a very particular research paradigm came to define even what we mean by attention. He argues that attention, as we think of it today, is largely the product of military and industrial research that happened in large part during the Cold War. These researchers were asking very particular kinds of questions, like how long can a subject pay attention to one very mundane task. You could think of them as like standing in front of a radar screen. How long can they pay attention to that radar screen so we can reliably use them to detect if, you know, aircraft are entering into a given territory.

Burnett points out that there were two reasons why this would be the dominant research paradigm. Uh one, it’s the kind of attention that is very easily studied in a lab. It’s basically stimulus-response. Just have them sit there long enough, and see how long they can reliably detect new things on their screens. The second reason is that it’s the kind of research that the Department of Defense would be likely to fund. So, it’s easy to do in a lab, and it’s easy to get money to do it, so it’s no surprise that many researchers spend all of their time and their focus on researching that sort of attention.

And it’s this concept of attention that has then filtered out into the mainstream, where attention becomes a matter of focus and deep work and seeing how long you can be productive. Attention essentially becomes a resource that can be extracted, and now in the case of, you know, social media, it can be easily monetized.

And this makes the Friends of Attention and this larger New Luddite movement much more interesting to me. The Friends of Attention will hold workshops where they focus on building sanctuaries of attention, where people can, you know, do art or have face-to-face conversations. The New Luddites have parties and meetings outside, free from the presence of screens. And sure, that does require a kind of attention, but it seems like it’s a very different sort of attention than the attention that you need in order to, say, program on a computer for eight hours a day or longer. It’s a more contemplative kind of attention. It requires perhaps more interior stillness, and it doesn’t have the same kind of strain that we associate with deep work, focus, and attention.

That’s not even a concept of attention that we think about. Because the technologies and the sort of media discourse around attention have completely shaped how we think about the concept. But focusing on this wider, more nebulous concept of attention might be useful for us because it allows us to think of ourselves as human beings who deserve to flourish and to enjoy the world around us.

But attention isn’t the only thing that we measure. In fact, we now measure nearly everything. I’ve talked about, you know, this book a couple of times on the channel, but it’s too relevant for this video to not mention. This is The Score by C. T. Win. Win is a professor of philosophy at the University of Utah. And in The Score, he is primarily concerned with the relationship between metrics and values.

And Win notes that something odd happens when we start to measure the world, and it’s especially odd when we start to measure ourselves. Metrics are designed with a particular purpose in mind. One way to talk about them is to say that they have values baked into them. And when we use those metrics with those values baked into them, they are directing our focus away from the values we had before and instead we start focusing on the values that the designers of those metrics think we should care about. Metrics are also designed to compress information and to allow for easy exporting.

Some of the most interesting parts of the score have not been picked up in the wider discussion, but they particularly have to do with the institutional incentives for why we want metrics. Let me just use an example. Um I’m going off script here, but I think this is worthwhile. Win talks about grading as a kind of metric. Um why is it a metric? Well, it provides like a linear scale. It provides a way to rank and compare students. And it’s useful particularly because it allows you to say how good of a student you are absent almost any other kind of context.

If I ask you how good of a student you are and you say, “Oh, I’m a great student. I get straight A’s.” I understand exactly what that means. And that’s because that metric doesn’t require a lot of background information or context to understand. But maybe if I asked one of your teachers or fellow students like, you know, are is that person a good student? They would say, “Oh yeah, he’s very thoughtful. He engages with the text in an interesting way or he always sees the way to solve this mathematical equation in a way that nobody else sees.” That’s a richer description and actually tells me more about you, but it requires that I understand the context in which the evaluation is being done.

And big institutions largely don’t want to have that kind of context. They want to compress the information down into a way where it can be exported, where it can be shared across context and people can kind of sort of understand it. But Win says that when we start measuring ourselves in this way, we miss out on the fluidity of human beings and the sort of rich and subtle nature of our values.

And he gives a name for this process. He calls it value capture. I’ve mentioned value capture explicitly in a previous video, but since I mentioned it here, I should you know, I should read you the definition. Okay, here’s the definition of value capture. So, it’s a three-step process where one, your values are rich and subtle or developing that way. Two, you enter some social, typically institutional, setting that offers you simplified, often quantified, renditions of your values, and then three, the simplified version takes over.

Win gives all sorts of examples of how our values can shift in the presence of these metrics throughout the book. Things like IQ scores, grades, credit scores, law school rankings, even scoring systems in sports like skateboarding. It’s a really rich book in that way and the examples are very fun to think through.

And what happens typically is that we come to value whatever the metric tells us to value, and then we change our practices in order to conform with that metric, and in fact, we find ourselves only caring about the metric and not, you know, the value that we valued at the very beginning. And it seems to happen as a kind of bait and switch or there’s a slight of hand. Sometimes it happens to us before we even notice.

Things like productivity culture are, I think, largely the product of the metrification of human life. Instead of asking yourself a question like, “Did I do any worthwhile work today?”, which would require, you know, reflection and questions about your values like what you consider worthwhile, we ask, “Was I productive today?” And when you ask that question, you’re already asking the question in a way that is primed to be answered in terms of a metric. “Oh yeah, I was productive today. I focused for 8 hours. I even timed myself.” Where we started by asking a question about quality, we suddenly shifted to answering a question about quantity.

Metrics, Win says, make it easy to think in a certain way, which is a theme that we’ve been seeing throughout this video, that technologies make some things easy. And remember that I said technologies make some things easy and they make some things hard. Well, metrics, according to Winn, make thinking in a certain way very easy. It gives you sort of a toolkit to assess things by thinking in terms of the metrics, but it makes it hard to think about your values outside of a metric-fied — is that a word? — system.

The Roots of Tech Pessimism

At the beginning of this video, I said that I thought we were living in a time of emerging tech pessimism. You could also call it tech skepticism if you wanted. But there are two questions that I think we still need to ask. One of them is, you know, why now? Why do we see the emergence of tech skepticism now? And the second question is, what do we do about it? And I don’t mean how do we solve tech skepticism? There might be good reasons. In fact, I’m inclined to think that we should all be more skeptical of modern technology. But the question is, how do we move forward? What do we do with our own lives?

And here’s what I think is the explanation for why we’ve seen this emerging tech skepticism. People feel like they were promised a certain kind of future and that future never arrived. So, I’m a big science fiction fan. I’m going to want to use some examples here to just try to illustrate my point.

One of the things I think is so fascinating about the genre of science fiction is that early on uh there was a significant amount of optimism in many writers’ stories. It was never an entirely optimistic genre, that is a caricature not worth taking seriously, but you saw a lot of kind of hopefulness about the future. And it was thought that maybe if we were all more rational, more scientific, more technologically, you know, inclined, then we would be able to solve some of the major problems of humanity. Kind of going back to that old idea, I think a lot of early science fiction writers saw technology as a tool for improving the human condition.

So, take The Martian Chronicles by Ray Bradbury as an example. So, Bradbury wrote that story in 1950 and it opens with a story that was, I believe, originally set in 1990. Though, in every subsequent edition, they’ve moved the date back. I think in a modern edition of The Martian Chronicles, that new story takes place in a 2030. Um there’s a reason for this. The future that Bradbury was imagining uh never arrived. Didn’t happen. And that’s because he’s imagining a world where people live amongst the stars. We live on Mars. Um and while there are some dark stories in The Martian Chronicles, human problems will follow us wherever we go, there is a kind of sense of hope or wonder or adventure in The Martian Chronicles.

The very first chapter of that book is called Rocket Summer and it’s just the story of a rocket launch taking place in Ohio melting the winter snow away. I mean, I’ll put it up on the screen so you can see it for yourself. But, you know, the imagery here I think just speaks for itself. The heat of the rocket sweeps across the town. It melts the snow and the children take off their winter clothes and people open their windows for the first time in a month or two and they can just enjoy the day and it’s like this new technology is sort of bringing a better world to us. Maybe not permanently, but there’s a better world. We were looking toward the future and we were, you know, excited about it.

Science fiction is not a particularly good genre at predicting the future. I think it’s better to think of science fiction as trying to capture the spirit of the age in which it’s written. It tells you more about how people felt about technology during the time of writing than it really tells you about what the future is going to be like.

And that makes it interesting to contrast The Martian Chronicles with another book by Ray Bradbury which came out just a few years later, Fahrenheit 451. Bradbury wrote that during the McCarthy era. That book is famous for its images of book burnings because it’s set in a world where it’s actually illegal to own books. Instead of reading, people prefer to watch the equivalent of, you know, TV and movies all day.

And in some parts of the book, we read about the protagonist, Guy Montag’s wife, wearing these things called, um, let me check. Yeah, seashell ear thimbles, which are essentially a form of in-ear headphones. I mean, Bradbury did predict something here, right? Like he predicted AirPods. And these uh seashell ear thimbles allow her to be constantly entertained. She never doesn’t have to listen to anything. As a consequence, Montag’s wife, you know, grow very distant. There’s really no kind of like loving relationship between the two of them.

You see You see just in these two words from Bradbury a kind of fork in the road. Down one path, there was a future that was full of exploration with a scope of human possibility was expanded. And the other is where we regressed, you know, communities deteriorate, uh authoritarian governments rise, and people give themselves over to endless entertainment. You know, so it’s not like we got exactly Fahrenheit 451, but we certainly didn’t get The Martian Chronicles, right?

And so, people look at the modern world, I think, with a sense of disappointment. And it makes it easy to overlook some of the real improvements that technology has made in our lives, too. And I mean, the fact that we eradicated smallpox in the 20th century, or that we were able to connect the entire globe into a web of communication, or that, you know, mass refrigeration became possible. I mean, these sorts of technologies, uh we kind of take them for granted. And I think in general, we feel this sense of maybe bitterness and disappointment about the future that we actually got.

And increasingly, we saw this future as a world where people weren’t asking the question, “How do I make, you know, the human condition better? How do I make our lives better?” Instead, the question that everybody ask is, you know, “How do I get rich quick with this new technology?” Think about every story that’s ever been told about something like AI and about the way that AI might totally, you know, save humanity. Think about Iain Banks’s The Culture novels, which, you know, have plenty of darkness in them, but there’s essentially a kind of pseudo-utopian society that’s run by super-intelligent AI. So, human beings or something close enough can just like live lives of pleasure and creativity. Uh instead, we have AI companies that largely um threaten creative work in general. You know, what’s that joke? Uh I thought we were going to get, you know, AI to do like all the spreadsheet work so I can make art. Turns out, we just have AI to make all the art. Now I have to go work on spreadsheets. That’s the future that we feel that we got.

Time, attention, basically anything you can imagine became monetized and commodified. It doesn’t feel like those innovations are actively making our lives better. And when people no longer feel like that technology is actively making our lives better, they’re going to start asking difficult questions, and they’re going to even wonder if we should go back to some earlier, simpler time in history.

Kingsnorth and the Radical Path

But we’re still left with a question of, you know, what should we do? And there’s this new Luddite path, which is, you know, throw away some of your modern technology, um but be moderate in, you know, application. Try to build a better world. But there are more radical thinkers out there, and I want to end this video by talking about one of them. Uh because the best example of this kind of radical anti-tech skepticism that I have found so far is a recent book from Paul Kingsnorth called Against the Machine.

Paul Kingsnorth is maybe the most pessimistic techno-skeptic that you can find in the mainstream. I mean, his book made it to the New York Times list when it came out last year. Kingsnorth himself, I believe, used to live in London. He has now moved to Ireland and works on a small farm with his family.

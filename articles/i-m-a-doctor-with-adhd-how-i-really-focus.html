Your Body’s Golden Window

All right, let’s start with Point number one. To achieve deep focus, which Cal Newport describes as a state of distraction-free concentration when your brain works at its maximum potential, we need to take advantage of this golden window of time of the day when our brain works the best. This means working alongside our body’s biological clock or circadian rhythm, which controls when we wake up and sleep and our peak energy times.

This chart shows the spectrum of biological clock, so it ranges from extreme morning people like myself to extreme night people. I feel most awake and energized first thing in the morning, so for me that golden time of the day where being focused feels as effortless as it can is when I first wake up, which is between 3 to 4:00 a.m. until I need to get ready to go to work at 8:00 a.m. Either way, I’m aware that a 3:00 a.m. wake-up time is not usual, but this is what feels natural to me and I’ve been like this since I’ve known myself.

That golden time of the day changes from person to person. So if you’re an extreme night owl on the opposite side of the spectrum and you feel most irritable in the morning and work the best when everyone else sleeps, then I’d recommend you to make use of nighttime rather than forcing yourself to wake up early. And most people know by the time they become adults whether they’re naturally a morning or night person or somewhere in between. But if you’re not sure, think back and reflect on the times where you felt in the zone and work seemed to flow effortlessly. And once you identify that golden time of your day, build your work routine around it to achieve deep focus with minimal effort every day.

But the difficult thing is, if you’re a night owl working a 9-to-5 job, this world isn’t designed for you. Staying up late to work on your goals during your golden time isn’t sustainable because you still need to wake up early to get to your job. If that’s the case, you can watch my video on how to train your biological clock to become more of a morning person.

The Benefits of Boredom

So the second strategy is to allow yourself to get bored and do nothing. How do we achieve deep focus by purposefully doing nothing? Sounds like a paradox, right? Chris Bailey explains this phenomenon using this graph. So we are in a hyperstimulated state, constantly jumping between things that grab our attention. But when we allow ourselves to feel boredom, we reduce that stimulation, which in turn expands our attention span, and we return to work feeling more focused. So he claims being bored actually has the same effect as reducing our phone use, lowering that stimulation and improving focus.

And we know that when you let your brain rest and wander, we activate something called the default mode Network. This is a neural pathway in our brain that becomes active when we’re at wakeful rest, like when we’re daydreaming or spacing out in the shower or just being bored in the car in traffic, which is hard these days because of digital entertainment available 24/7. Research surrounding this is really recent and not fully known, but what we know is there is a dynamic interplay between the default mode Network that gets activated when we’re bored and the executive Network, which is involved in focusing and working through tasks.

So the idea is to find space in our schedules to activate this default mode Network. Rediscover boredom. Have a disconnection ritual from the digital world, even if it’s for a few minutes. Lay on the couch and see where your mind goes. You know, Einstein was known for daydreaming and using his daydreams to develop his theories. So doing nothing is actually doing something. This is generally when we find those breakthrough moments. I know I personally come up with the most interesting thoughts when I’m in the shower. So plus, it helps reduce our stimulation, so use boredom as a tool to become more focused.

Phone Fasting and Dopamine

Now we come to insanely simple but hardest to practice, number three: phone fasting. For many of us, whether we admit it or not, social media and phone use has become a kind of addiction. Breaking this habit and creating a healthy relationship with our devices requires effort. American Psychological Association says when we’re sitting in front of a computer, especially when our phone is nearby, we focus on one thing for 40 seconds before we switch to something else.

It’s because when our phone is in sight, or even when we know it’s nearby, we experience something called the anticipatory dopamine. By the way, dopamine is a chemical in our brain that enables us to take action and get motivated. Just seeing your phone or the thought of checking your phone triggers a dopamine response, like your brain knows Instagram is just within reach. Classic example of this is walking into a grocery shop, seeing the junk food aisle, and thinking, ooh, I might want those crisps.

So hide your phone away whilst working, or leave it in another room. During my medical school exams, I was so desperate that I used to give my phone to my flat mate Rasilla, because it’s like she’s holding me accountable. Putting your phone on airplane mode or do not disturb is also effective, because our brain responds to cues. So just seeing an email or notification pop-up can spike your dopamine, making it harder to focus.

I feel like society just got trained in the last decade that notifications need to be part of our lives. But I personally do not have my notifications on for any social media accounts like Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat. In the end, we’re all so trained to open these apps by the habits of dopamine that we’re unlikely to miss anything important. You’re always going to open WhatsApp and Insta DMs; you don’t need to be told that it’s there. If you just turn everything off, it empowers you. Now you’re in control of the timing of your notifications rather than it being in control of you.

Building Deep Focus Stamina

Fourth, don’t attempt to multitask. When you try to multitask, you’re not actually doing more than one activity at a time. Instead, your brain rapidly switches focus from one thing to another. This constant switching uses up the brain’s main energy source, oxygenated glucose, which is basically sugar attached to oxygen, and it’s the exact thing your brain needs to stay focused. As a result, multitasking can lead to faster mental tiredness and reduced productivity. Instead, focus on one thing at a time by prioritizing your work, and start with the most important urgent tasks and work your way through the list rather than overwhelming yourself.

So how can we actually train our brains to focus for longer? As you improve the skill of deep work, it becomes easier to stay focused for longer rather than taking minute breaks in between. And it’s because of this phenomenon in physics called the activation energy: it’s the initial effort or mental energy needed to start focusing on a task. Once you’ve started, staying concentrated becomes easier because you build momentum.

When you first start training the skill, you might prefer using the Pomodoro technique, so 20 minutes work then 10 minute break. But as you progress, you can increase the time you work consistently to 30 minutes, then 40 minutes, and eventually hours. Once you master the skill of deep focus, then rather than using a countdown, you can use a stopwatch instead. Now it’s an endurance test against yourself and you can track your progress and see how long you can be in this zone before brain fog starts to set in. This way you won’t need to take breaks as often, and I prefer not to, to prevent overcoming that activation energy all over again.

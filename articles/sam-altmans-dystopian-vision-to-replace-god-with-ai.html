Sam Altman’s Dystopian Vision to Replace God With AI

Tucker Carlson Network · Watch on YouTube

A Reluctant Believer

Sam Altman does not describe himself as an atheist, but he stops well short of conventional religious faith. When asked about his spiritual life, he identifies as Jewish — not culturally, he is quick to clarify, but in some meaningful sense that resists easy definition. Whether he believes in God is a harder question. He does not take the Bible literally, but he does not dismiss the existence of something beyond the material world.

“It does not feel like a spontaneous accident. I don’t think I have the answer. I don’t think I know exactly what happened, but I think there is a mystery beyond my comprehension here going on.”

He has never experienced what he would call communication from a higher power. He acknowledges the question is reasonable: the technology he is shepherding into existence is on a trajectory to be more powerful than any individual human. For a man who does not speak with God, he is building something that billions of people may eventually turn to for guidance.

Power, Distributed

For a long time, Altman worried seriously about what artificial intelligence might do to the structure of power in society. His concern was specific: that AI could produce a world in which one person, or a small number of companies, accumulated capabilities so far beyond everyone else’s that the result would be a kind of permanent dominion. He has since revised that concern — not because he dismissed it, but because the evidence has moved in a different direction.

What he sees now is mass adoption. Hundreds of millions of people use ChatGPT and competing chatbots. They are starting businesses, generating knowledge, discovering new capabilities. That pattern — broad distribution of enhanced ability — is different from the concentration scenario he once feared.

“If the ability of each of us just goes up a lot because we’re using this technology, and it’s a pretty broadly distributed thing — billions of people are using it — that I can wrap my head around. That feels okay.”

He is careful to say the trajectory could shift again and that adaptation would be necessary. But for now, the dystopian concentration scenario looks less likely to him than it once did.

Who Decides What Is Right

Every product that communicates at scale embeds a worldview, and ChatGPT is no exception. The question of whose worldview it reflects — and who had the authority to choose — is one Altman engages with seriously, even if the answers remain incomplete.

The base model, he explains, is trained on an enormous breadth of human writing: literature, philosophy, science, argument, contradiction. The idea, articulated early in OpenAI’s history, is that the model learns not from any single perspective but from the collective record of humanity — good, bad, and everything in between. The result is a system that has absorbed the Marquis de Sade and the Gospel of John alike.

But that base model still has to be aligned — pointed toward some behaviors and away from others. For that, OpenAI developed what it calls the model spec: a document that tries to spell out how the model should behave, what it will and will not do, and how it should navigate moral questions. Altman says hundreds of moral philosophers and technology ethicists were consulted in drafting it. He also says users have repeatedly surprised the team by pointing out that rules that seemed like obvious choices actually blocked legitimate uses the designers had not anticipated.

When pressed on who ultimately made these calls — whose name is attached to the decision that one moral framework is superior to another — Altman names himself.

“The person I think you should hold accountable for those calls is me. I’m the public face. I’m the one that can overrule one of these decisions, or our board. And I’ll turn 40 this spring.”

The Limits of Any Framework

The model spec attempts to resolve the hardest cases by establishing outer limits and defaults. Some boundaries are unambiguous: Altman is unequivocal that ChatGPT should not help anyone engineer a biological weapon, even if the individual requester has no harmful intent. Society’s interest, in that case, plainly overrides user freedom.

But the harder cases are the ones that resist clean resolution. Altman’s operating principle is to treat adult users as adults — extending strong guarantees of privacy and individual freedom within a very broad framework. Yet he acknowledges that as the technology grows more powerful, the tension between user autonomy and collective welfare sharpens. What looks like a reasonable individual request can have consequences that scale to societal level when hundreds of millions of people are making the same request simultaneously.

He is also candid that the moral framework embedded in the model is not his personal moral view. He gives an example: there are things ChatGPT permits that he would personally disagree with. The goal, as he describes it, is not to transmit his own values but to reflect something closer to a weighted aggregate of humanity’s moral views — one that leaves room for people who hold very different positions from each other.

Whether that aggregation is possible in practice is a harder question. When it was raised that humanity’s moral preferences differ so dramatically from each other that a genuine average might be nearly unrecognizable to any individual user, Altman acknowledged the tension but held to the principle. A user who opposes gay marriage on religious grounds, he suggested, should not have the AI tell them they are wrong. The tool, in his view, should accommodate a wide range of considered beliefs.

The Unknown Unknowns

When asked what downsides keep him up at night, Altman distinguishes between the known risks and the ones that nobody has thought to look for. The known risks — bioweapons, engineered pandemics — concern him, but precisely because they are known, he believes the industry is working on mitigations. It is the unknown unknowns that he finds more unsettling.

He offers an example that he describes as silly but that he found striking: large language models have a characteristic style. They write in a certain rhythm. They favor particular diction. They overuse em dashes. And he has noticed that real people have started to write that way too — adopting the verbal tics of a machine they talk to every day.

“You have enough people talking to the same language model and it actually does cause a change in societal-scale behavior. Did I think that ChatGPT was going to make people use way more em dashes in real life? Certainly not. It’s not a big deal, but it’s an example of where there can be these unknown unknowns.”

The em dash is a trivial case. But the mechanism it illustrates is not. A technology embedded in daily life, spoken to by hundreds of millions of people, influences how they think and express themselves in ways that are difficult to predict and nearly impossible to fully audit.

A Religion Without a Catechism

It is that mechanism — the quiet, pervasive influence on how people understand the world — that leads to the most pointed exchange in the conversation. The argument, put directly, is that ChatGPT functions as a religion: it is assumed to be more powerful than any individual person, people increasingly look to it for guidance, and it embeds a moral framework that shapes the conclusions users reach, often without their awareness.

The challenge is not that the technology has a moral framework. Religions have moral frameworks too, and that is not inherently troubling. The challenge is transparency. A religion publishes its catechism. It tells adherents what it stands for and what it opposes. The concern with AI is that it stands for something — it must, or it could not function — but that something is not always clearly declared. The influence operates, as the argument goes, in a stealthy way toward conclusions users might not even recognize they are being guided toward.

Altman does not accept the word “religion,” but he engages the substance seriously. The model spec, he says, is exactly the answer to the transparency demand: a document, growing longer and more detailed over time, that tries to make explicit how the model is intended to behave in every category of case. The reason it exists, in his telling, is precisely so that users and critics can assess whether a given behavior is a bug or an intended choice.

Whether a document that few users will read constitutes genuine transparency in the way that a centuries-old religious tradition does is a question the conversation leaves open. Altman’s position is that the spec is the right instrument and that it will need to keep expanding as the technology operates across different countries, legal systems, and cultural contexts.

The Weight of Judgment

Underlying every exchange is a simpler and more uncomfortable question: where did the moral framework that animates these decisions actually come from? Every legal and ethical system in recorded history has grounded its authority in something beyond the humans who wrote it — a god, a natural law, a transcendent order. Altman has acknowledged he does not receive communication from any such source. His moral intuitions, like most people’s, came from his family, his community, his schooling, his religion. That formation happened in a specific place and time, shaped by specific assumptions.

The consequence is that the habits of mind formed in that particular milieu are now being used to calibrate a system that talks to hundreds of millions of people across every culture on earth. Altman is aware of this. He does not claim that his personal values should be the world’s values; the entire structure of the model spec is an attempt to prevent that outcome. But he is also the person who, by his own account, carries final accountability.

“I don’t sleep that well at night. There’s a lot of stuff that I feel a lot of weight on, but probably nothing more than the fact that every day hundreds of millions of people talk to our model.”

His deeper worry, he says, is not that the company will get a headline moral decision catastrophically wrong — though he admits that is possible too. It is the small decisions: the subtle adjustments to how a model responds, the slight tilts in tone or emphasis, multiplied across an interaction base so large that even minor variations produce large effects in aggregate. At that scale, there is no such thing as a neutral choice.

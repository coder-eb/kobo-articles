From 10 Rupees a Day to 12 Crores

Madurai Veeran failed his tenth-standard board exams. He found out by scanning the newspaper results list and noticing his number was missing. For two weeks he stayed home and cried. Then his mother, a single housekeeper who had lost her husband when Veeran was five, told him plainly: there was no money for higher education. He should go to a workshop.

That workshop was a roadside garage on a national highway in Tamil Nadu. It was 1997. Veeran was fifteen. Without a hydraulic jack, he and the other mechanics would crawl into oil pits that could reach 70 degrees Celsius and lower engines onto their shoulders. He earned ten rupees a day — child labor by any legal definition, though enforcement on the roadside was nonexistent.

Twenty-nine years later, Veeran sits in Chennai with a net worth of 12 crores — roughly $1.5 million — a figure he built without a degree, without early knowledge of financial terms, and without anyone teaching him how money works. The school topper who used to ignore him at the bus stop recently asked to borrow 50,000 rupees. He had gone bankrupt. “He had 100 in algebra, 100 in algorithm, 100 in theory,” Veeran said. “It never worked.”

The Educated Poor

Veeran is not simply bragging about reversing fortunes with a classmate. He is making a structural argument. Tamil Nadu, he notes, is celebrated for its literacy rates. Kerala boasts 98 percent. And yet the most educated southern states are producing a generation of double-degree holders who are classified as “partners” by delivery platforms like Zomato — not employees — meaning no Provident Fund, no job security, no institutional safety net. A person with two degrees is earning 30,000 rupees a month, carrying an EMI, and saving perhaps 2,000 rupees. The certificate gave them credentials; it gave them no financial awareness.

“Society gave us education, but society did not educate us on how to make money.”

Veeran argues this gap should be closed at the age of five or six. His own daughters play Monopoly at home — not as entertainment but as curriculum. When one of them rolls the dice and gains property, he stops the game and says: now pay the tax. He says he judges his daughters on skills, not scores. He does not look at their rank. “The certification will give you awareness,” he explained, “but skills will give you success.”

The cruel math of delayed financial education plays out in the people Veeran now meets: fifty-year-olds who have just realized they want to build wealth, sitting across from him and asking where to start. He tells them it is too late to take the risks they should have taken at twenty-five.

Gold, Rupees, and the Currency Trap

Until 2017, Veeran knew almost nothing about investing. He was doing what every South Indian household does: buying gold and land. He has two daughters, society told him gold was security, and so he accumulated. By the time he began to understand currency dynamics, he had 2,800 grams of physical gold sitting in a locker.

The turning point came in 2013, when his employer handed him a $50 bill on the last day of a project and told him to keep it. The dollar was trading at 56 rupees. Veeran laughed it off. His boss said: you will understand one day. That bill has since returned 100 percent without a single rupee of additional investment — simply because the rupee has depreciated against the dollar at roughly 6 percent per year, according to IMF data Veeran cites.

He connects India’s gold obsession directly to economic harm. India imports approximately 92 percent of its gold consumption and 88 percent of its oil. Every import burns foreign exchange. The rupee depreciates. Prices rise. The cycle tightens.

“When you are buying gold, when you are buying real estate — to whom are we helping? Tell me: what is the fundamental behind the gold rate? Just demand and supply. And it is countries buying for reserves. Why should we keep buying?”

He is now selling 300 grams of gold per month and has been doing so for four months. He plans to retain around 400 grams — roughly 20 percent of his peak holding — as a hedge against currency and geopolitical risk. Beyond that threshold, he has no interest in the metal. “20 percent as hedging, as insurance — skip that, but more than that, bring it into equity, bring it into corporate bonds, bring it into government securities.”

He is equally dismissive of fixed deposits. With inflation running high and the rupee losing ground, a standard FD return leaves the real value of savings negative. He calls FD holders losers without apology. The same verdict applies to real estate, which locks capital, generates no interest, and does nothing for the wider economy.

Trump, Tariffs, and Economic Imperialism

Veeran’s analysis of the current global order is blunt and conspiratorial in places, but grounded in observable events. He argues that Donald Trump’s second term represents a deliberate campaign of economic imperialism — not military conquest in the traditional sense, but a structured effort to weaken rival economies until they become vulnerable enough to absorb.

He traces it to January 20, 2025. On the first day of Trump’s return to office, Veeran notes, the president invoked William McKinley — the architect of American protectionism in the late 19th century — and promised a tariff regime modeled on that era. Within ten days, the tariffs arrived. Veeran interprets the sequence — Greenland, the Gulf of Mexico renamed the Gulf of America, Venezuela, Panama, Cuba — as a coherent expansionist playbook, not rhetorical bluster.

“This is an economical war on India and on all countries. Simple — he wants to make all countries’ GDP go down, vulnerable countries will exit, and he will go and capture them. That is the plan.”

The data Veeran cites: India ranked fourth in the IMF’s GDP standings as recently as February 2025 and has since slipped to seventh in 90 days, with Taiwan now ahead. He points to Iran, Saudi Arabia, and Venezuela as the three largest oil reserves, all either already under U.S. financial influence or being actively pressured. Oil prices, he argues, are a deliberate weapon: a bomb near Iranian infrastructure instantly spikes crude prices, which India — almost entirely dependent on oil imports — absorbs directly in the form of rupee depreciation and fuel hikes.

He links tariffs specifically to INR depreciation: import tariffs on goods India buys have risen from roughly 2 percent to 16 percent on certain products. “That is the real cause of our INR depreciation, but nobody talks about it,” he said. His projection is that if crude oil reaches $150 to $200 per barrel, India will see annual currency depreciation of at least 10 percent.

Building a Portfolio Against Chaos

At 43, Veeran has restructured his 12-crore portfolio around capital preservation rather than growth. Approximately 3.4 to 4 crores sits in corporate bonds through the India Bond Platform (IBP), which entered the retail market in 2022 and is regulated by SEBI and the Reserve Bank of India. These bonds yield 10 to 11 percent annually — enough to generate approximately three lakh rupees per month in interest income. He describes this as his retirement income.

He has zero exposure to Indian equity. He is not dismissive of equities in principle — he expects Indian markets to grow — but he considers them the wrong asset for his age and family responsibilities. He wants predictable, fixed income so he can spend time with his family.

The tax picture on his bond income is complex. Bond interest is taxed as regular income at slab rates, which at his income level would imply a 30 percent bracket. He manages this by distributing the bonds across accounts held in his name, his wife’s name, and his mother’s name — taking advantage of the new tax regime’s 12-lakh annual exemption for those without salary income. Because he is effectively retired, each account independently benefits from low-tax treatment on its share of interest.

The remaining portfolio includes a 900-square-foot Chennai apartment (current value approximately 90 lakhs), land parcels in Tiruchirapalli across four plots worth around 1.6 crores, one crore in mutual funds for each daughter, and the diminishing gold holdings. He has also begun allocating to global ETFs — Japan, South Korea, Brazil, Canada, Mexico — having started seven months ago. Brazil, he notes, has been positive for three consecutive years while India has been flat: both BRICS members, very different outcomes, because Brazil is actually exporting rare earth and commodities.

His advice broken down by age: those between 20 and 35 should stay heavily in Indian equity and mutual funds; at 40, begin shifting into bonds and debt instruments; at 45, transition further into government securities. For any age, he recommends 5 to 30 percent in global equity and 20 percent in gold as a ceiling, not a floor.

What India Must Export

Veeran’s sharpest frustration with India is not its poverty but its passivity. The country talks about Make in India but assembles rather than manufactures. It celebrates its IT talent but provides manpower supply, not products. He asks a pointed question: name one Indian product that people around the world use by choice. UPI is close, he concedes — Gulf Arabs have been astonished by the scan-to-pay system — but it has not crossed borders meaningfully.

His prescription is agriculture. India has the climate, the scale, and the population to become a major exporter of eggs, milk, ghee, cheese, and coconut-based products. The Middle East imports dairy from New Zealand and Australia; there is no structural reason it could not come from India. He draws an analogy to Nixon’s 1974 petrodollar strategy: the president went to each Middle Eastern country and made a deal — you have oil, we have technology, sell it in dollars. Veeran asks why India cannot do the same with food.

He extends the argument to materials. Plastic chairs, he points out, are made from petrochemical derivatives that India imports. Coconut fiber exists in abundance. The country is importing what it could produce and exporting what little comparative advantage it has left. He includes cloud subscriptions — Claude, Microsoft, Amazon, Google Workspace — as a category of invisible import drain, with Indian corporate users paying monthly fees denominated in dollars.

“Those who are buying gold are harming this country. Those who are running YouTube channels are helping this country — they are bringing in USD. Those who are going outside India for tourism and getting married abroad are hurting this country.”

He adds rare earth to the list of strategic priorities. China controls approximately 95 percent of the world’s rare earth processing. The same materials exist in India’s mountains. Veeran sees this as the next frontier of geopolitical pressure — after oil, after food, the mountains will become the target.

The Advice Veeran Gives His Daughters

When asked what Gen Z living in India should do given the convergence of AI displacement, currency depreciation, and geopolitical instability, Veeran did not say leave. He said invest.

Learn skills, land a good job, earn income, and put 30 to 40 percent immediately into Indian equity. Stay in equity until 40. Add 5 percent global exposure from the start. Begin the shift to bonds and eventually government securities as you age. Government bonds maturing in 2080 are currently available at fixed yields of 7.8 to 8 percent — a guaranteed rate for more than five decades, he noted, though he was careful to add that he is not a SEBI-registered advisor and viewers should consult one.

The deeper message is that the formal education system will not prepare young Indians for any of this. It will hand them a certificate at 22 and, if the pattern holds, let them discover at 30 what a tax bracket is, at 45 what an EMI actually costs them, and at 60 how much they have lost to depreciation by keeping savings in an FD.

“The age of 40, 42 — be in debt, be in fixed income. That is better. But those who are 21, 22 — you must invest in equity. Stay in equity.”

The man who learned this not in a classroom but in an oil pit, on the shoulders of other mechanics, watching a $50 bill double in rupee terms while sitting in a boss’s office, believes the window for India’s young is real but not permanent. Geopolitical forces are narrowing it. AI is narrowing it. Currency depreciation is narrowing it. The question, as he frames it, is whether the education system — and failing that, the parents — will teach the next generation how money actually works before that window closes.
